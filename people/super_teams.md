# Characteristics of 'Super' teams

- Purpose and meaning is derived from being a part of the team itself, because it makes them better
- Runs more experiments (example: A/B testing), up to 48% more. This fosters innovation
- Embraces continuous improvement as a never ending pursuit
- Members receive and deliver way more feedback than in average teams. Not just top-down but peer-to-peer. Feedback is built into how the work gets done, not just at the end of a project.
- Making mistakes are normalised, encouraging experimentation and risk-taking
- Sees progress as the goal, not perfection
- Focuses on what needs to change, not what went wrong
- Are deliberate about how time is spent at work. (example: Meeting free days, fixed meetings are periodically re-evaluated for impact) 
- Members disconnect from work in their off hours. But how off-hours are spent determine performance at work. Members spend off-hours in mastery experiences, not passive activities like resting or social media. 
- Leaders encourage team members to challenge themselves outside their teams - moonlighting on other projects, side gigs, and hobbies
- Leaders are 'focus amplifiers'; frees team members from distractions and allow them time to focus on what matters
- Leaders are receptive to learning from people they lead. 
- Leaders ask the question: what are you stuck on? This brings people together to solve problems. It's normal to feel stuck when doing hard things
- Leaders are hands on, investing in the day to day operations. Helps the team feel a deeper sense of collaboration. Have a clear view of ground reality, helping them spot challenges and opportunities way faster than hands-off leaders
- Not everybody is suitable for super teams. Requirements: open mindedness, willingness to learn, willingness to try new things, adaptable, and open to continuous improvement.
